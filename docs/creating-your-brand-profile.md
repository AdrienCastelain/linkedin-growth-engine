# Creating Your Brand Profile

> **What this is:** The complete guide to filling in your brand profile. There are two versions — minimal (15 minutes, 4 sections) and full (30 minutes, 10 sections). This doc covers both, with explicit guidance on the section that matters most: voice samples.
>
> **Why this matters:** The brand profile is the per-brand customization layer. The rulebook handles universal content rules. The brand profile handles everything that varies across brands: positioning, voice, pillars, story bank. A weak brand profile produces generic content no matter how good the rulebook is. A strong brand profile makes the system feel like it knows you.

---

## Which version should I use?

**Use the minimal version ([`templates/brand-profile-minimal.md`](../templates/brand-profile-minimal.md)) if:**
- You're trying the system for the first time
- You want to ship a batch this weekend, not next month
- You haven't yet decided whether you'll commit to a weekly cadence
- You don't have a content audit, story bank, or benchmark accounts ready

**Use the full version ([`templates/brand-profile-template.md`](../templates/brand-profile-template.md)) if:**
- You've already shipped 1-2 batches with the minimal version and you're staying with the system
- You have time to think strategically about pillar weights, story bank, and benchmark accounts
- You want the workflow to run in Returning Mode (which uses the full profile fields)

**You can always upgrade.** Minimal → full is a one-way door, and the migration is easy: copy the full template, paste in everything from your minimal version, fill in the new sections.

## The minimal version — 4 sections

### Section 1: Positioning

> "I help [WHO] do [WHAT] so they can [OUTCOME]."

One sentence. The hardest sentence in the document.

**Examples that work:**
- "I help pre-Series A SaaS founders build their first repeatable GTM motion so they can stop guessing what's working and start scaling what does." (specific ICP, specific outcome)
- "I help indie game developers ship their first paid title so they can get out of side-project purgatory and into a real career." (specific ICP, specific outcome)
- "I help mid-career engineers transition into engineering management so they can lead the teams they were tired of complaining about." (specific ICP, specific outcome with personality)

**Examples that don't work:**
- "I'm a fractional CMO." (job title, not positioning)
- "I help businesses grow." (too generic — what businesses, grow how)
- "I bring strategic thinking and execution excellence to high-growth teams." (consultant-speak with no specifics)

**The test:** Read your sentence to a friend who doesn't work in your industry. Ask them: "Could you describe what kind of person should hire me?" If they can answer in one sentence, it works. If they hedge, your positioning is too vague.

**Time:** 5-15 minutes if you're figuring it out as you go. 60 seconds if you already have it written somewhere.

---

### Section 2: LinkedIn Goal

Pick exactly one. The full list is in the rulebook (§4.1) and includes 8 options. The 3 most common for solo operators:

- **Inbound leads.** You want DMs from prospects asking to work with you. Drives Type A and Type C closings.
- **Thought leadership.** You want to be cited, invited to podcasts, and asked to speak. Drives Type A and Type B closings.
- **Grow audience.** You want to build a following from a low base. Drives Type A closings.

**Don't pick more than one.** "Inbound leads AND thought leadership" sounds reasonable but produces incoherent content because the two goals optimize for different post structures, different closing types, and different reply behaviors. Pick the one that matters most this quarter.

**You can change it later.** Most users start with "grow audience" and switch to "inbound leads" or "thought leadership" once they have 1,000+ followers.

**Time:** 60 seconds.

---

### Section 3: Content Pillars

3-4 topics you can credibly talk about for the next 12 months. Each pillar gets a one-line description.

**The "10 posts" test:** Could you write 10 posts about each pillar from your own experience without making anything up? If not, the pillar is too narrow. Widen it.

**Common mistakes:**
- **Pillars are too narrow.** "Pricing for vertical SaaS" might give you 3 posts. "Pricing and positioning for early-stage B2B" gives you 30.
- **Pillars are too broad.** "Marketing" is not a pillar. "Founder-led marketing for early-stage SaaS" is.
- **Pillars overlap too much.** "GTM strategy" and "Go-to-market motion" are the same pillar.
- **Pillars are off-positioning.** A fractional CMO whose pillars include "AI tooling for designers" is going to confuse their audience.

**Pillar weights:** The minimal version skips weights — defaults are applied (40/30/20/10 for expertise/personal/engagement/promotional). The full version lets you set custom weights.

**Time:** 5-10 minutes if you're thinking through them. 2 minutes if you've already done the strategic work elsewhere.

### Section 4: Voice Profile

This is the section that matters most. **Read this whole section before filling it in.**

#### Why voice samples are everything

The system needs to learn your voice. It can't infer it from your positioning or your job title — it has to see actual writing. The 5 reference samples in §5c are how the system learns:

- Your formality level (casual vs. precise)
- Your sentence rhythm (short and punchy vs. long and flowing)
- Your contractions habit
- Your conversational markers (do you use "honestly," "look," "yeah"?)
- Your em dash frequency
- Your signature phrases
- Your willingness to swear, joke, admit uncertainty
- How you open paragraphs
- How you handle transitions
- Whether you use specific numbers or general framings

Without samples, the system defaults to generic AI voice. *Specifically, it defaults to LinkedIn-bro voice* — short paragraphs, polished closings, manufactured vulnerability, motivational poster lines. This is the failure mode you're trying to escape. Voice samples are how you escape it.

#### What counts as a voice sample

**Good:**
- A LinkedIn post you wrote yourself (not a draft a contractor wrote for you)
- A long email or Slack message in your natural professional voice
- A blog post excerpt
- A podcast transcript (paste the parts where you're talking)
- A reply on Twitter/X, Reddit, or Hacker News
- A long comment on someone else's post
- A Notion page where you wrote something for yourself, not for an audience

**Bad:**
- Anything written by a marketer, ghostwriter, or AI on your behalf
- Job descriptions, product copy, or About pages (too polished, too rehearsed)
- DMs in a register you wouldn't use professionally (too casual)
- Anything you "wrote with AI's help" — it has to be unassisted

**Length:** 100-300 words per sample, ~1,000 words total across 5 samples. Shorter is fine if that's all you have. Longer is fine if the sample is one coherent piece.

**How many:** 5 is the target. 3 will work. 0 will refuse to run.

#### How to find samples fast

**The "open three tabs" method (10 minutes):**
1. Open your LinkedIn profile → Activity → Posts. Find 1-2 of your best posts. Copy them.
2. Open your work email → Sent → search for the 3-4 longest emails you wrote in the last month to a peer (not a client, not a vendor, a peer). Copy them.
3. Open your Slack or Discord → search your own messages → find 1-2 substantive messages where you explained something or pushed back on someone. Copy them.

Five samples in ten minutes. This is the fastest path.

#### What to do if you don't have samples

If you genuinely have no writing samples (maybe you've never published, or you're rebranding):

1. **Draft 5 yourself, right now.** Write a 200-word "what I think about [topic in your pillar]" piece for each of your 4 pillars, plus one more on a related topic. They don't need to be polished. They need to be in your voice.
2. **Paste them as your samples.** The system learns from these.
3. **Replace them as soon as you have real published content.** After Batch 2 or 3, swap in your actual published posts as the new samples.

This is a cold-start workaround, not the recommended path. Real samples beat fabricated ones every time.

#### The "always avoid" list

3-5 words you would never naturally use. The rulebook already bans the obvious ones (delve, leverage, harness, etc. — see §2.2). The "always avoid" list is for words specific to your niche or your taste:

- **Niche jargon you hate.** A SaaS GTM expert might ban "playbook," "north star," "flywheel." A designer might ban "pixel-perfect," "delight," "hand-off." A writer might ban "narrative arc," "voice and tone," "story-driven."
- **Words you've noticed yourself flinching at.** When you read other people's posts, what words make you wince? Add them.
- **Words from your previous marketing.** If a previous marketer wrote in your voice and used phrases that aren't actually you, ban them.

This list is a force multiplier on the universal rulebook. The rulebook catches the AI tells everyone shares. Your "always avoid" list catches the niche-specific tells that make you sound like every other consultant in your space.

**Time for Section 4 total:** 10-15 minutes if you have samples ready. 30+ minutes if you have to hunt. Set a timer.

## The full version — 6 additional sections

The full template adds Sections 5-10 on top of the minimal. Quick guide to each:

### Section 5: Voice Profile (extended)

The minimal version captures only reference samples and the avoid list. The full version adds:
- **Formality slider (1-10)** — your number plus a one-sentence description of where you sit
- **Sentence rhythm** — short / mixed / flowing
- **Em dash calibration** — count em dashes in your samples, set the per-post limit (0, 1, 2, or 3)

These are tuning knobs. They give the agent more guidance, but they're optional — the agent infers all of them from the samples themselves if you skip these fields.

### Section 6: Hook & Closing Preferences per Pillar

Default hook style and closing type per pillar. The agent uses these as starting points for each post. Without them, the agent rotates through defaults.

**When this is worth filling in:** After Batch 2 or 3, when you've noticed which combinations work best for which pillars. Before then, defaults are fine.

### Section 7: Story Bank

5-10 real situations from your work. This is the second-most-important section after voice samples.

**Format per story:**
- **Title** — short label
- **Pillar** — which content pillar this fits
- **Theme** — failure / success / surprise / lesson
- **Summary** — 2-3 sentences (what happened, what was at stake, what you learned)
- **Details** — specific numbers, names, timeframes — the things that pass the rulebook's Colleague Test (Gate 8)

**Why it matters:** The rulebook's Ground Rule 1 bans fabrication. If a post needs a story you didn't provide, the workflow either uses your story bank or pauses to ask you. Without a story bank, every post that needs a story triggers a pause.

**The right ratio:** Aim for at least 2 stories per pillar. Lean toward failure and surprise stories — they outperform tidy success stories on LinkedIn because they pass the Colleague Test more easily.

### Section 8: Benchmark Accounts

5-10 LinkedIn profiles you learn from, compete with, or want to learn from. Used by Module 10 (Competitive Intelligence) and Module 11 (Reshare Strategy).

**Selection criteria** (from rulebook §10.1):
- 2-3 peers (5K-50K followers)
- 2-3 aspirational targets (50K-500K)
- 1-2 pattern sources (500K+) — for inspiration, not direct comparison

**When to fill this in:** Before your first Returning Mode batch. Modules 10 and 11 are advanced — you don't need them in your first month.

### Section 9: Target Market

Drives which cultural profile loads from Module 19 (US, UK/EU, ANZ, APAC, or global mix). One field, takes 30 seconds.

**Default to US** if you're unsure. Switch later if you notice your content lands flat in your actual audience's market.

### Section 10: Include / Avoid Topics

Two lists:
- **Topics you WILL talk about** — your declared lane
- **Topics you will NOT talk about** — politics, competitors by name, anything off-position

This list feeds Gate 9 (Topic Consistency Check). Posts outside your "will talk about" list get flagged as off-pillar.

## Common mistakes to avoid

1. **Skipping voice samples or pasting only 1-2.** This is the #1 reason the system produces generic content. Section 5c is the only HARD STOP gate. Treat it accordingly.

2. **Filling in placeholder positioning and forgetting to come back.** The agent uses your positioning sentence to filter topic candidates. If your positioning is `[FILL IN]`, you'll get generic topics.

3. **Picking too many pillars.** Three is enough. Four is the maximum. Five splits your content into directions the algorithm can't recognize as "you."

4. **Story bank with no specific details.** "I helped a client improve their conversion rate" is not a story. "A founder client's signup form had a 'company size' dropdown with the smallest option being '10-50 employees.' 43% of solo founders bounced" is a story. Specificity matters.

5. **Avoid list with only generic words.** The universal rulebook already bans "delve," "leverage," "transformative." Your avoid list should be niche-specific. If your list overlaps 100% with the rulebook, you're not adding value.

6. **Treating the brand profile as a one-time task.** Your voice evolves. Your pillars shift. Your story bank gets stale. Refresh the profile every 90 days. Add new samples. Add new stories. Update the avoid list with words you've started flinching at.

## Running this for multiple brands

If you manage LinkedIn content for more than one brand — your own plus 1-3 client retainers, or an agency setup — the brand profile system supports it directly. The pattern is one folder per brand, and each brand folder has its own `_user/` subfolder containing that brand's state.

**The pattern:**

```
my-clients/
├── my-own-brand/
│   └── _user/
│       ├── my-brand-profile.md
│       ├── my-content-audit.md
│       ├── my-industry-research.md
│       └── my-content-agent.md (optional)
├── client-a/
│   └── _user/
│       ├── my-brand-profile.md
│       ├── my-content-audit.md
│       └── my-industry-research.md
└── client-b/
    └── _user/
        └── ...
```

To run a batch for a specific brand, navigate to that brand's folder (`cd my-clients/client-a/`) and invoke Claude. The bootstrap detects `_user/` in your current directory, loads that brand's profile, and runs the workflow against it. Each brand has its own voice samples, story bank, pillars, and avoid list — no cross-contamination between brands.

The LGE skill is installed once (in `~/.claude/skills/linkedin-growth-engine/` for Claude Code, or any convenient location for other runtimes). The rulebook and workflow are shared across all brands — one copy of the skill, N copies of brand-specific state.

**The hard rules:**

1. **Never share a brand profile across brands.** Voice drift is guaranteed. Each brand needs its own §5c reference samples.
2. **Never share a story bank across brands.** A story from Client A's work cannot show up in Client B's content. If you accidentally cross-pollinate, you've broken the Colleague Test for both brands.
3. **Never share an agent identity across brands.** If you're using the agent layer (Module 6), build one agent per brand. The agent's voice protocol enforces "load this brand profile, write as this brand." Sharing an agent across brands defeats the protocol.
4. **Each brand's "always avoid" list (§5d) is independent.** What "playbook" means to a SaaS founder is not what it means to a designer. Don't copy avoid lists between brands.

**Time impact:** managing 3 brands is roughly 3x the steady-state weekly time, so ~1.5 hours per week instead of 30 minutes. The setup time is ~3x as well, so ~3-4 hours of brand profile work distributed across the first month. Compared to writing all that content yourself, it's a massive saving. Compared to a tool that ignores voice, it's slower but produces work you'd actually publish under your clients' names.

**For agencies:** the maintenance moat (the rulebook updating monthly) compounds across all your clients simultaneously. Update once, every brand benefits. This is the strongest argument for using one system across multiple clients instead of bespoke approaches per client.

## After your first batch

Once you've shipped 1-2 batches with the minimal version, come back to your brand profile and:

1. **Notice what the agent got wrong.** Words it used that weren't yours. Lines that didn't sound like you. Add them to your avoid list.
2. **Add 1-2 more voice samples.** The 5 samples you started with were a guess. After a batch you know which ones were closest to "the voice you want." Add more in that direction.
3. **Start your story bank.** Even if you don't upgrade to the full template yet, keep a running list of real situations you can reference next batch.
4. **Decide if you're upgrading to the full template.** If you're committing to 4+ batches, upgrade. If you're still evaluating, stay minimal.

The brand profile is a living document. Every batch teaches you something about it.
