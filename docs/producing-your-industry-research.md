# Producing Your Industry Research

> **What this is:** A guide to filling in [`templates/industry-research-template.md`](../templates/industry-research-template.md) — a monthly snapshot of what's happening in your industry that the workflow uses in Step 2 (Topic Selection) to make sure your weekly batches tie into current conversations instead of recycling evergreen takes that feel disconnected from the moment.
>
> **Who this is for:** Returning Mode users (typically month 2+). First-Run Mode users skip this entirely — Step 2 in First-Run Mode runs from your pillars alone.
>
> **Time:** 30-45 minutes per refresh, monthly.

---

## Why you need it

Without industry research, your content is purely evergreen. That's not a disaster — evergreen content can work — but it has two failure modes:

1. **Posts feel timeless in a bad way.** Readers can tell when content was written 3 months ago and is "still relevant." It signals you're not paying attention to what's happening in your space right now.
2. **You miss timing-driven angles.** A post about pricing during the week your competitor raises prices is 5x more shareable than the same post in a normal week. Without research, you miss these moments.

The industry research file is the structured input that turns "I think this is a good topic" into "I know this topic ties into something my audience read this week."

---

## When you need it

**You don't need industry research in First-Run Mode.** First-Run Mode skips the research filter in Step 2. Your first batch runs from your pillars alone.

**You need it in Returning Mode** (typically from month 2). The workflow's prerequisites block requires a research file that's <30 days old.

**Refresh cadence:** Monthly. Set a recurring 45-minute calendar block for the first Monday of each month and treat it like any other ritual.

---

## What to research

The template has 7 sections. Each one answers a question about what's happening in your space right now.

### 1. What's happening in your industry this month

3-5 bullets. The conversations your ICP is actually having. What's trending in their LinkedIn feed, in industry newsletters, in podcast episodes they listen to.

**Where to find this:**
- **Your benchmark accounts' recent posts.** What are April Dunford, Lenny Rachitsky, your peers actually posting about this month? If 3 of them are talking about the same thing, that's a trend.
- **Industry newsletters.** SaaStr, Lenny's Newsletter, OnlyCFO, Stratechery, your niche-specific newsletter — what are they covering this month?
- **Podcasts your ICP listens to.** Listen to or skim transcripts of 2-3 recent episodes. What topics keep coming up?
- **Twitter/X.** If you use it, search for keywords from your pillars and see what's being argued about this week.
- **Your own DMs.** What are clients and prospects asking you this month? Your DMs are an underrated source of trend signal.

**Time:** 15-20 minutes if you already follow the right sources. 60+ minutes if you don't.

**What to write down:**
- The trend in one sentence
- Where you saw it (newsletter, account, podcast — be specific)

### 2. New data, studies, or reports

Anything published in the last 30 days that your ICP would care about. These become hooks for "number" style posts (rulebook §4.2).

**Where to find them:**
- Industry research firms (Gartner, Forrester, IDC for B2B; CB Insights for venture; Pitchbook for funding)
- Independent researchers (Lenny's Newsletter often publishes original survey data; ProductLed; OpenView)
- Academic papers if your niche is technical
- Government data if relevant (BLS, Census, etc.)

**What to write down:**
- Source
- Headline finding (one sentence)
- Date published
- Link

**Goal:** 2-3 strong data points per month. Not 10. Two specific studies you can cite are more useful than 10 you mention in passing.

### 3. Hot takes circulating

Opinions, debates, or controversies your ICP is reacting to. These are the contrarian-hook opportunities.

**Where to find them:**
- LinkedIn comment sections on your benchmark accounts' posts (the comments often reveal the debate)
- Twitter/X arguments in your niche
- Hacker News threads on industry topics
- Newsletter sections where the writer takes a position and the comments push back

**Selection rule:** Only include hot takes where you have a real, defensible position. If you're "kind of agreeing with the consensus" — skip it. The point is to find takes you can write contrarian posts against.

**What to write down:**
- The take (one sentence)
- Where it's circulating
- Where you stand on it (and why)

### 4. Tools, products, or launches worth mentioning

New tools your ICP might be evaluating. Product launches in your space. Integrations that change the workflow.

**Where to find them:**
- Product Hunt
- Industry-specific launch boards
- Newsletter "what's new" sections
- Twitter/X — "we just launched X" posts

**Why this matters:** Useful for first comments and reshare opportunities. A post about product positioning that mentions a real recent launch feels current. A post that doesn't feels generic.

### 5. What your benchmark accounts are posting about

For each of the 5-10 benchmark accounts in your brand profile (Section 8), pull their recent themes. You're looking for:
- What topics are getting traction in your niche right now
- What gaps you can fill
- What's being said too often (avoid)
- What's not being said but should be (lean in)

**Where to find this:**
- Open each benchmark account's profile → scroll their recent posts (last 2-4 weeks)
- Note the recurring themes
- Note which posts had high engagement vs. low engagement

**Time:** 5-10 minutes per benchmark if you skim. Don't read every post.

**Gap analysis:** What's NOT being covered that your ICP would care about? Those are your highest-leverage post topics.

### 6. Specific events, conferences, or calendar anchors

Anything time-sensitive in the next 30 days that's relevant to your audience.

**Examples:**
- Conferences (SaaStr, Web Summit, Money 20/20, etc.)
- Industry awards
- Regulatory deadlines (e.g., GDPR, SOC2 audits)
- End-of-quarter rituals (Q1 board meetings, fiscal year planning)
- Product launches you know about in advance
- Holidays your audience cares about

**Why this matters:** Timing posts to anchors gives them context. A post about pricing strategy the week after a major SaaS conference is more relevant than the same post in a random week.

### 7. Topics to tie in this month

The synthesis section. The workflow will use this list directly when generating topic candidates in Step 2.

Three lists:

**Strong candidates** (topics where you have a real angle and the timing is right):
- 5+ topics ready to go

**Weaker candidates** (topics worth tracking but not posting yet — wait for a stronger angle or a developing story):
- 2-3 topics to revisit next month

**Topics to actively avoid this month** (saturated, off-pillar, or politically risky):
- 1-2 topics to skip

If you can't fill the "strong candidates" section with 5+ topics, you didn't research deeply enough. Go back to Section 5 and dig into what your benchmarks are actually saying.

## The 45-minute monthly research ritual

Block 45 minutes once a month. Use this sequence:

### Minutes 0-10 — Benchmark account scan
Open each of your 5-10 benchmark accounts. Scroll their last 2-4 weeks of posts. Note the 3 most-discussed themes per account.

### Minutes 10-20 — Newsletter and podcast scan
Open the 2-3 industry newsletters you read. Skim the headlines and intros from the last 4 issues. Note any data points, hot takes, or new tools mentioned.

### Minutes 20-30 — Data and studies hunt
Search for "[your industry] report 2026" or "[your niche] study" on Google. Find 2-3 recent studies with usable findings. Note the source and headline.

### Minutes 30-40 — Synthesis
Open your industry research file. Fill in sections 1-6 from your notes. Don't try to be comprehensive — capture what stood out.

### Minutes 40-45 — Topic candidate list
Fill in section 7. List 5+ strong candidates, 2-3 weaker ones, 1-2 to avoid. This is the most important section for the workflow.

**If you go over 45 minutes,** you're being too thorough. Cut. The research file is a snapshot, not an analysis.

## What you don't need to do

- **You don't need to read every newsletter every week.** Once a month is fine. The point is to capture trends, not stay current daily.
- **You don't need to write a literature review.** 3-5 bullets per section is enough.
- **You don't need to predict the future.** The research is about what's happening right now, not what will happen.
- **You don't need to find original insights.** You're cataloguing what others are saying. Your *posts* will add the original angle.
- **You don't need to do this for every batch.** Monthly is enough. The same research file feeds 4 weekly batches.

---

## Common mistakes

1. **Researching for too long.** This becomes procrastination. Set the 45-minute timer and respect it. The research file is good enough at 30 minutes.

2. **Trying to be exhaustive.** You can't catalogue everything happening in your industry. You're sampling. The 5-10 benchmark accounts are a representative slice — that's enough.

3. **Including takes you don't have a position on.** Section 3 is for takes you can write *against*. If you're "kind of in the middle," skip the take. Wait for one you have an actual opinion on.

4. **Filling in section 7 without really synthesizing.** This is the section the workflow reads. If it's vague, the topics are vague. Treat it as the deliverable.

5. **Letting the file go stale.** A 60-day-old research file gives the workflow stale signals. Refresh monthly without exceptions.

6. **Researching topics outside your pillars.** Stay in your lane. A trending topic that's outside your declared pillars is not relevant to you. Don't try to broaden your content because you noticed something interesting elsewhere.

## How the workflow uses this file

In Step 2 (Topic Selection), the agent reads your research file and uses it as a filter:

1. **Section 1 (industry trends):** The agent prefers topic candidates that tie into a current trend over candidates that are timeless.
2. **Section 2 (new data):** The agent uses these data points as hooks for "number" style posts.
3. **Section 3 (hot takes):** The agent suggests contrarian topics aligned with your stated positions.
4. **Section 5 (benchmark themes):** The agent identifies gaps — topics not being covered by your benchmarks that fit your pillars.
5. **Section 7 (synthesis):** The agent prioritizes the topics in your "strong candidates" list before generating new ones.

The better your research file, the sharper your Step 2 output. The worse the file, the more generic the topics.

---

## Before you ship a Returning Mode batch

Quick checklist:

- [ ] Section 1 has 3-5 trends with cited sources
- [ ] Section 2 has at least 2 data points from the last 30 days
- [ ] Section 3 only includes takes you actually have a position on
- [ ] Section 5 covers at least 5 of your benchmark accounts
- [ ] Section 7 has 5+ strong topic candidates
- [ ] Last refreshed date is <30 days ago

If any item is incomplete, fix it before running the workflow. Step 2 depends on this file being current and useful.
