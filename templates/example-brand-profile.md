# Brand Profile — Alex Hayes (Example)

> **Note:** This is a fictional example. "Alex Hayes" is a stand-in for an early-stage SaaS fractional CMO. Use it as a reference when filling in your own brand profile. The voice samples and stories are fabricated to show what well-formed entries look like, not to be reused.

---

## 1. Positioning

> I help pre-Series A SaaS founders build their first repeatable GTM motion so they can stop guessing what's working and start scaling what does.

---

## 2. Ideal Customer Profile (ICP)

- **Role:** Solo or co-founder of an early-stage SaaS company. Usually technical, occasionally product-led, rarely sales-led.
- **Company stage:** Pre-seed to Series A. $0-2M ARR. 2-15 employees. No full-time marketer yet.
- **Primary pain point:** They've shipped a product that works but they can't tell which growth channel is actually moving the needle. Every month feels like a fresh experiment with no compounding learning.
- **Where they are right now:** They've tried content, ads, cold outbound, and SEO. Some of it kind of worked. None of it worked enough to bet the company on. They need someone to look at their actual data and tell them what to keep, what to kill, and what to double down on — without selling them a 6-month engagement they can't afford.

### 5c. Reference samples

> **Note for new users:** Sample 2 below and Story 1 in Section 7 are the same engagement seen from two angles — a real Slack message and the structured story-bank entry for the same moment. This is encouraged. One real engagement can feed multiple template fields, and pulling raw voice from your actual messages is the fastest way to calibrate this section.

**Sample 1 (LinkedIn post from Q4):**
> A founder asked me last week which growth channel they should pick. They had $8K/month and 6 weeks before their next board meeting.
>
> Wrong question.
>
> The right question is: which channel can you debug fastest if it doesn't work? Because most early-stage growth experiments don't work the first time. The channel that lets you see why it's failing is the one that compounds.
>
> SEO takes 6 months to read the signal. Cold outbound takes 4 weeks. Founder content takes 2. Paid takes 7 days.
>
> If you have 6 weeks, you have exactly one channel that can give you a real answer. Pick the one with the shortest debug loop, not the one with the highest theoretical ceiling.

**Sample 2 (Slack message to a founder client):**
> ok so i looked at the funnel data — the leak isn't where you think it is. it's not the pricing page, it's the field on the signup form that asks for company size. 43% of solo founders bounce there. they're self-selecting out because they think the product isn't for them. fix: make the field optional, add a "just me" option at the top of the dropdown. should take you 20 mins.

**Sample 3 (Email reply to a podcast invite):**
> Thanks for the invite. I'm in for the recording on the 14th. One ask: I'd rather not do the "tell us your story" intro segment if that works for you. I find those segments lose the audience and we waste 8 minutes that could be tactical. Happy to say two sentences about who I work with, then dive into the actual conversation.
>
> Also — your last episode with the Linear founder was the best founder marketing conversation I've heard this year. Pricing as positioning is the right hill.

**Sample 4 (Blog post excerpt):**
> The myth I want to kill is that "founder-led marketing" means the founder posts on LinkedIn three times a week. It doesn't. That's a tactic, not a strategy.
>
> Founder-led marketing means the founder is the source of truth for what the product does, who it's for, and why it matters — and that everything the company says publicly traces back to that source. The CEO writes the homepage headline. The CEO records the demo video. The CEO answers the first 100 sales calls. The CEO writes the first 50 blog posts.
>
> Once you have those artifacts, you can hand them to a marketer and they have something to scale. Without them, every marketer you hire reinvents the company's story from scratch. That's why the first marketing hire so often fails.

**Sample 5 (Twitter / X reply to another founder):**
> The thing nobody tells you about fractional work: the first month is almost always spent undoing the last marketer's dashboard. Not because they were bad. Because the KPIs they picked made sense for the stage the company was at when they joined, and the company isn't there anymore. If you take a fractional role, budget the first 4 weeks for archaeology, not output.

## 7. Story Bank

**Story 1:**
- **Title:** The signup form that killed conversion
- **Pillar:** GTM strategy for early-stage SaaS
- **Theme:** Surprise
- **Summary:** A founder client's signup form had a "company size" dropdown with the smallest option being "10-50 employees." 43% of solo founders bounced at that field. They were self-selecting out because they assumed the product wasn't for them. Fix took 20 minutes. Conversion went from 12% to 31% in two weeks.
- **Details:** Pre-seed B2B tool, $0 → $400K ARR in 9 months post-fix, founder is now hiring a head of growth.

**Story 2:**
- **Title:** The renewal I talked my client out of raising
- **Pillar:** Category positioning
- **Theme:** Lesson
- **Summary:** A founder client wanted to raise prices 40% at renewal because a competitor had just repriced. I talked her down to 15%. Six months later I found out two of her three biggest accounts would have paid the 40% without blinking. I'd anchored on competitor data instead of her customers' willingness to pay. She left ~$180K on the table that year because I was cautious on her behalf.
- **Details:** Still bothers me. Two of her enterprise accounts were ~$350K ACV each, the 25-point delta between 15% and 40% was roughly $175K-$180K over the year. Pricing decisions should be grounded in customer research, not competitor benchmarks. I now run a 4-call willingness-to-pay check before any pricing change over 10%.

**Story 3:**
- **Title:** The "founder content" plan that almost killed a CEO
- **Pillar:** Founder-led marketing
- **Theme:** Failure
- **Summary:** First time I worked with a fractional client, I wrote them a content plan: 4 posts a week, 30 min each. By week 6 they were burned out, behind on product, and starting to resent me. I'd built a content plan for a marketer, not a founder. Killed it, replaced it with 1 post a week + a 90-min monthly recording session that got chopped into clips. Engagement went up.
- **Details:** Lesson: founder time is the constraint, not founder ideas. Build for the constraint or the system collapses.

**Story 4:**
- **Title:** The pivot that didn't need a pivot
- **Pillar:** GTM strategy for early-stage SaaS
- **Theme:** Surprise
- **Summary:** A founder told me they needed to pivot because growth had stalled at $1M ARR. I asked to see the cohort data. Their NRR was 142%. They didn't need a pivot, they needed to charge more on renewal. Took two weeks to implement. ARR jumped to $1.6M without a single new customer.
- **Details:** B2B vertical SaaS, retention was the lever the whole time. Founder had been so focused on new logos he'd never looked at expansion.

**Story 5:**
- **Title:** The blog post that took 40 hours and got 14 readers
- **Pillar:** GTM strategy for early-stage SaaS
- **Theme:** Failure
- **Summary:** Spent 40 hours on a deeply researched piece about pricing strategy for vertical SaaS. Published it. 14 readers. 3 of them were from my own team. Lesson: at small scale, SEO doesn't reward depth. It rewards being findable, and a brand new domain isn't findable. Should have written 5 short LinkedIn posts on the same insights instead. When I finally did, 2 months later, the reach was ~80x the blog post.
- **Details:** This is why I stopped recommending SEO to anyone under $1M ARR. Not because SEO is bad, but because the feedback loop is too slow to learn from at that stage.
