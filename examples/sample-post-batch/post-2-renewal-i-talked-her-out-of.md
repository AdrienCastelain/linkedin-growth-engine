## The post (as it would publish)

> A founder client wanted to raise her renewal prices 40% last spring. A competitor had just repriced and she wanted to follow.
>
> I talked her down to 15%.
>
> Six months later I sat with her enterprise CSM and went through the renewal data. Two of her three biggest accounts would have paid the 40% without blinking. The third was on the fence at 25%.
>
> The math: those two accounts were ~$350K ACV each. The 25-point delta between 15% and 40% was roughly $175K-$180K over the year. I'd cost her almost two hundred grand by being cautious on her behalf.
>
> Here's the part that still bothers me. I wasn't being conservative. I was anchoring. The competitor's pricing felt like data, so I treated it like data. It wasn't data. It was a single observation about one company's risk tolerance, dressed up as market intelligence.
>
> Real pricing data lives inside her customers' willingness-to-pay conversations. I never had those conversations before I gave her the 15% number. I should have spent two days on willingness-to-pay calls and zero days on competitor research.
>
> Now I run a 4-call willingness-to-pay check before any pricing change over 10%. Doesn't make me right. Makes me less anchored.
>
> Maybe the worst pricing mistakes are the ones where the consultant thought she was being careful.

---

## First comment

> The competitor-pricing trap is everywhere in early-stage SaaS. It's the easiest data to find, so it's the data that gets used. Willingness-to-pay calls are harder, slower, and so much more accurate that the difference is embarrassing. If you're a founder reading this and you've never asked five customers what they'd pay if they had to pay double, that's your weekend assignment.

---

## Reply template (for substantive comments)

> The honest answer is I think I was protecting myself, not her. A 15% raise feels safe to recommend. A 40% raise feels like I'm responsible if it goes wrong. The willingness-to-pay calls would have made it her decision, not mine. That's probably what I was avoiding.

## What this post demonstrates

**The critic loop matters.** The first draft had a closing line that would have shipped if the generator was running alone. The critic caught it as a parallel construction and forced a rewrite. The rewritten close is more honest, less marketable, and more memorable — which is the test the rulebook is built around.

**Real failure outperforms manufactured vulnerability.** The post makes Alex look worse, not wiser. She lost her client almost $200K. The reply template doubles down by admitting she was protecting herself. This is what the rulebook means by Section 2.3's "manufactured vulnerability" rule — the opposite of which is *earned* vulnerability with no tidy resolution.
