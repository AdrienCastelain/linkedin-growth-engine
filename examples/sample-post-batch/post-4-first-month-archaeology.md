## The post (as it would publish)

> Started with a new client this week. Sat down to look at her growth dashboard for the first time. Spent two hours not knowing what I was looking at.
>
> The dashboard wasn't broken. It was a perfectly built dashboard for a company that no longer exists. The KPIs made sense for the stage she was at when her first marketer joined. She's not at that stage anymore. The marketer left four months ago. The dashboard kept running.
>
> This is the part of fractional work nobody warns you about: the first month is almost always archaeology, not output.
>
> Not because the previous marketer was bad. Because the company changed faster than the measurement system did, and now there are 14 metrics on the dashboard and only 4 of them describe the business as it exists today.
>
> What I do in week 1 of any new fractional engagement now:
>
> 1. Pull every dashboard, every spreadsheet, every monthly report from the last 12 months. Every one.
> 2. For each metric, ask: what decision did this metric inform? Is that decision still being made?
> 3. Kill the metrics that nobody's used to make a decision in 60 days.
> 4. Add the metrics for the decisions that are being made now without any data.
> 5. Tell the founder what I'm killing and why, before I kill it.
>
> Steps 1-4 take a week. Step 5 takes another week because founders are emotionally attached to their old metrics. They built them. They picked them. Killing them feels like criticizing the founder's past judgment.
>
> If you're considering a fractional CMO and you're worried they'll be slow to ship: budget the first 4 weeks for archaeology, not output. Anyone who promises you Week 1 deliverables is going to scale a measurement system that's already wrong.
>
> What's on your dashboard right now that nobody's looked at in 60 days?

## First comment

> The version of this that's hardest is when the founder built the dashboard themselves. Killing a metric they made feels like saying "your judgment was bad." It wasn't. It was right for the company they had then. Different company now. Different metrics. The conversation is awkward and unavoidable.

## Reply template (for substantive comments)

> Yeah the 60-day rule is just a practical heuristic. Some metrics matter even if you only check them quarterly (cohort LTV, expansion rate). The right question isn't "how often is this checked" but "what decision does this inform." If the answer is "none, we just like having it," it's archaeology fodder.
